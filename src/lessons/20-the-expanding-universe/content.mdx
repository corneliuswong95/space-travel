For most of history people assumed the universe was static and eternal. In the 1920s that picture collapsed. Wherever astronomers looked, galaxies were rushing away from us — and the farther a galaxy was, the faster it fled. Wind that motion backwards, and everything in the universe was once packed together.

## The galaxies are flying apart

The decisive clue is in the light. When a galaxy moves away, its light is stretched to longer, redder wavelengths — an effect called <Term def="The stretching of light to longer, redder wavelengths when its source is moving away from us. The faster the source recedes, the greater the redshift.">redshift</Term>. Edwin Hubble found that almost every galaxy is redshifted, and that more distant galaxies are redshifted more. The universe is not standing still; it is expanding.

## It is space itself that stretches

Here is the part that defies intuition: the galaxies are not flying through space away from some central point. Space itself is stretching, and carrying the galaxies along with it.

<Aside>
Picture raisins in a rising loaf of bread. As the dough expands, every raisin drifts away from every other one, and the farther apart two raisins are, the faster they separate. No raisin is the centre — each one would see all the others receding. Galaxies in the expanding universe behave the same way.
</Aside>

<KeyIdea>
The Big Bang was not an explosion at one place in space, hurling matter outward into a void. It was the beginning of space itself, expanding everywhere at once. There is no centre to the universe and no edge it is expanding into — the stretching happens between all points, everywhere, all the time.
</KeyIdea>

## Rewinding to the Big Bang

If the universe is expanding, it was smaller, denser, and hotter in the past. Follow that logic all the way back and you arrive at a beginning — the <Term def="The event about 13.8 billion years ago when the universe began expanding from an extremely hot, dense state. It marks the start of space and time as we know them.">Big Bang</Term>, about 13.8 billion years ago. It was not a bang in any ordinary sense, and not a thing detonating into emptiness; it was the start of the expansion we still measure today.

## The afterglow we can still detect

The early universe was so hot it glowed. As it expanded and cooled, that ancient light was stretched out until it became faint microwaves — the <Term def="The faint glow of heat left over from the early universe, now stretched into microwaves and arriving from every direction in the sky. It is the oldest light there is.">cosmic microwave background</Term>, the oldest light in existence, still reaching us from all directions. It was discovered by accident in 1965 as an unexplained hiss in a radio antenna, and it remains the strongest evidence that the universe truly began hot and dense.

<FunFact>
You may have seen the Big Bang's afterglow yourself. On an old television tuned between channels, a small fraction of the static — the random snow on the screen — was the cosmic microwave background: the heat of the newborn universe, picked up by the aerial.
</FunFact>

## The dark majority

For all we have learned, most of the universe remains a mystery. Ordinary matter — every star, planet, and person — makes up under 5% of it. About a quarter is dark matter, the unseen mass that holds galaxies together. The rest, roughly 70%, is <Term def="A mysterious form of energy that appears to fill empty space and is driving the expansion of the universe to speed up. Its nature is unknown.">dark energy</Term>, which is pushing the expansion to accelerate. We have named these two, but we still do not know what either of them is.

<FunFact>
The expansion has no speed limit. Space can stretch faster than light, so the most distant galaxies are receding faster than light can travel toward us — which means their light will never arrive. The universe we can see is only a bubble inside a far larger whole we can never observe.
</FunFact>

## How far we can see

Because light moves at a finite speed and the universe has a finite age, we can only see objects whose light has had time to reach us. That bubble is the <Term def="The part of the universe we can in principle see — everything close enough that its light has had time to reach us in 13.8 billion years. The whole universe is larger, perhaps infinitely so.">observable universe</Term>. Strangely, it is about 93 billion light-years across — far wider than 13.8 billion light-years — because space kept expanding while that ancient light was still in transit.

<KeyIdea>
Run the universe backwards and it began hot, dense, and tiny 13.8 billion years ago, then expanded into everything we see. The evidence is concrete: galaxies racing apart, and the faint afterglow of that first heat still glowing in every direction. The cosmos has a history, and we can read it.
</KeyIdea>

But there is something this whole picture has skated past. The galaxies, the light, the atoms we are made of — all of it is only a sliver of what the universe contains. Most of it is dark, and unaccounted for. That is what we confront next.
