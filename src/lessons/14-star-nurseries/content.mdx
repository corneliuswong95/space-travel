Stars can feel permanent, but they are not. They are born, they age, and they die — and birth is happening right now, in places you can point a telescope at tonight. To understand the stars, it helps to start where every one of them begins: in a cold, dark cloud.

## The cold clouds between the stars

The space between the stars is not empty. It holds thin gas and dust, and in places this material gathers into vast, cold, dark <Term def="A giant cloud of cold gas and dust between the stars. Its dense cores are where new stars form; the Orion Nebula is a nearby example.">molecular clouds</Term> — some large enough to build thousands of stars. These clouds are the raw material of every star, including the Sun.

## Gravity pulls a clump together

Where part of a cloud is dense enough, gravity takes over and the clump begins to collapse, growing hotter as it falls inward. Often something gives it a push — the shockwave from a nearby exploding star can squeeze a cloud and set the collapse going. The dense, heating core at the centre is a <Term def="A star still forming: a hot, dense clump of collapsing gas that has not yet ignited fusion in its core.">protostar</Term>.

## A star and its planets, born together

As the cloud falls in, it spins faster and flattens into a disc around the protostar — the same process that shaped our own Solar System. The star builds at the centre, and the leftover material in the disc clumps into planets. Stars and their planets are born from a single event, which is a large part of why planets turn out to be so common.

## The star switches on

When the core finally reaches about 10 million °C, fusion ignites, and the protostar becomes a true star. The young star's light and wind blow away the leftover gas around it, and it settles into the long, steady life described in the next lesson.

## Born in litters

Because the clouds are so large, they rarely make just one star. They light up whole batches at once — <Term def="A group of stars that formed together from the same cloud and are roughly the same age. Some, like the Pleiades, are easy to see; over time a cluster slowly drifts apart.">star clusters</Term> of siblings that share an age and slowly drift apart over millions of years.

<FunFact>
The Sun was born in a cluster of perhaps thousands of stars, all formed from the same cloud. They long ago scattered across the galaxy, so somewhere out there drift the Sun's siblings — stars made from the very same batch of gas, now lost among the crowd.
</FunFact>

<FunFact>
The Orion Nebula, where thousands of stars are taking shape right now, is faintly visible to the naked eye as the fuzzy middle "star" of Orion's sword — a stellar nursery you can find yourself on a dark winter night.
</FunFact>

<KeyIdea>
Stars form when gravity collapses cold clouds of gas and dust, usually many at a time, each protostar wrapped in a disc that can grow into planets. Star birth and planet birth are the same event — which is why the galaxy is full of both.
</KeyIdea>

A newborn star settles into a long life of fusion. How that life unfolds — and how it ends, quietly or in a catastrophe — depends above all on one thing: the star's mass.
