Mars is the most studied world beyond our own — a cold, rust-coloured desert about half the width of Earth. More robots have landed there than anywhere else in the Solar System, for one reason: of all the planets, Mars looks most like a place that could once have been alive.

## Why Mars is red

The colour is rust, almost literally. Martian soil is rich in iron, and over billions of years that iron reacted with oxygen to form <Term def="Iron oxide — the same compound as common rust. A fine layer of it coats much of the Martian surface and tints the whole planet red.">iron oxide</Term>. Wind lifts the fine dust into the air, giving Mars a butterscotch daytime sky rather than a blue one.

## A smaller, colder, thinner world

Mars is about 6,800 km across, and its gravity is just over a third of Earth's — a person who weighs 60 kg here would weigh about 23 there. Its atmosphere is thin — under 1% of the pressure at Earth's surface — and almost entirely carbon dioxide. With so little air to hold heat, the average temperature sits near −65 °C, and liquid water cannot survive in the open: it would freeze or boil away almost at once.

<FunFact>
Sunsets on Mars are blue. The same fine dust that makes the daytime sky butterscotch scatters light in the opposite way to Earth's air, so the glow that lingers around the setting Sun is a cool blue — the reverse of the red sunsets we are used to.
</FunFact>

## The largest volcano and deepest canyon

Mars holds some Solar System records. <Term def="A shield volcano on Mars about 22 km tall — roughly two and a half times the height of Mount Everest — and as wide as a country at its base.">Olympus Mons</Term> is the tallest volcano known, and Valles Marineris is a canyon system some 4,000 km long, enough to stretch across a continent. With no drifting tectonic plates to break them up, Martian landforms grew to enormous size and stayed put.

<Readout label="Olympus Mons · height" value="22" unit="km" />

<Aside>
Olympus Mons is so broad — about the size of France — and its slopes so gentle that if you stood at its base, the summit would be hidden below the horizon. You could climb the tallest volcano in the Solar System and never see a peak.
</Aside>

## Water, then and now

Mars is dry today, but it was not always. Orbiters and rovers have found dried-up riverbeds, deltas, and minerals that only form in water — evidence that billions of years ago Mars had lakes and flowing rivers beneath a thicker atmosphere. Much of its water is now locked in the polar caps and underground. That lost wet past is exactly why we keep returning: where there was water, there may once have been life.

## Storms that swallow the planet

Mars has weather, and at its worst it is planet-wide. Every few years, dust storms can grow until they wrap the entire globe and blot out the surface for weeks. These storms are thin — the air is far too sparse to knock anyone over — but the dust they raise blocks sunlight. In 2018 a global storm cut off the solar-powered Opportunity rover, ending a mission that had been meant to last 90 days and had run for nearly 15 years.

## Two small moons

Mars has two tiny, lumpy moons, Phobos and Deimos, most likely captured asteroids. Phobos orbits so close and so fast that it laps the planet quicker than Mars turns, rising in the west and setting in the east — twice a day.

The next time Mars hangs bright in the night sky, you are seeing sunlight bounced off a rusty desert that once ran with rivers. Beyond it, the planets change character entirely: no more solid ground, only the giants.
