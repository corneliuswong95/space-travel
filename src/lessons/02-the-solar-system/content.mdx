The Solar System is the Sun and everything bound to it by gravity: eight planets, their moons, dwarf planets such as Pluto, and countless asteroids and comets. It formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a collapsing cloud of gas and dust — and almost all of that material ended up in a single place.

## The Sun makes the rules

The Sun holds about 99.8% of all the mass in the Solar System. Everything else — every planet, moon, and asteroid combined — is the remaining 0.2%. That dominance is why everything orbits the Sun, and why a closer planet must move faster to hold its orbit: Mercury laps the Sun every 88 days, while Neptune needs 165 years.

## Two families of planets

The eight planets fall into two clear groups. The four inner planets — Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars — are small, dense balls of rock and metal. The four outer planets — Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune — are giants made mostly of gas and ice, with no solid surface to stand on.

The split is not an accident. Close to the young Sun it was too hot for ices to survive, so only rock and metal could clump together, building small worlds. Beyond a boundary called the <Term def="The distance from the young Sun beyond which it was cold enough for water, ammonia, and methane to freeze into solid ice.">frost line</Term>, ice was plentiful, so planets there grew massive enough to also capture thick atmospheres of gas.

## The distances are larger than they look

Space is mostly space. To keep the numbers manageable, astronomers measure distances inside the Solar System in <Term def="One astronomical unit (AU) is the average Earth–Sun distance: about 150 million kilometres.">astronomical units</Term>, where the Earth's distance from the Sun is exactly one.

Notice how the inner four crowd close to the Sun while the outer planets are flung far apart. Neptune orbits thirty times farther out than Earth. Sunlight reaches us in eight minutes; it takes more than four hours to reach Neptune.

## The sizes vary just as much

The planets differ as dramatically in size as in distance. Jupiter is so large that every other planet could fit inside it with room to spare.

Set against the giants, the rocky worlds we tend to picture first — Earth included — are barely a sliver. It is a useful correction: planets like ours are the small exceptions, not the rule.

## The order, and what comes next

From the Sun outward: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune. Each is a world with its own history — extreme heat, lost oceans, storms larger than planets — and the lessons ahead visit them one at a time.
