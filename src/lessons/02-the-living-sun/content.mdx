In the first lesson the Sun was a steady furnace, converting mass to light at a constant rate. Seen up close it is nothing so calm. Its surface boils, its magnetic field knots and snaps, and it flings storms of particles across the Solar System — some of which arrive here. The Sun is not a fixed lamp but a living, variable star, and we orbit inside its reach.

## A boiling, magnetic surface

The visible surface, the <Term def="The Sun's visible surface layer, where the gas becomes transparent and light escapes. It is not solid, but a churning layer of plasma about 5,500 °C.">photosphere</Term>, is not smooth. It seethes with granules — the tops of columns of hot gas rising, cooling, and sinking again, each the size of a country. Because the Sun is a ball of electrically charged plasma that spins faster at its equator than its poles, this churning winds its magnetic field into ever more tangled knots. Almost everything the Sun does beyond simply shining traces back to that magnetism.

## Sunspots and the 11-year cycle

Where the magnetic field grows especially strong, it chokes off the flow of heat rising from below. That patch of surface cools to around 4,000 °C — still fiercely bright, but cooler than its 5,500 °C surroundings, so it looks dark by contrast. This is a <Term def="A cooler, darker patch on the Sun's surface where the magnetic field is strong enough to slow the heat rising from below. Sunspots come and go over an 11-year cycle.">sunspot</Term>.

Sunspots are not random. Their number swells and fades over an 11-year rhythm called the solar cycle, as the Sun's magnetic field builds up, tangles, and eventually flips over entirely.

## Flares and coronal mass ejections

When those tangled field lines snap and reconnect, they release energy stored over months in seconds. A <Term def="A sudden, intense burst of radiation from the Sun, released when tangled magnetic field lines snap and reconnect.">solar flare</Term> is the flash of radiation; often it comes with a <Term def="A coronal mass ejection: a huge cloud of magnetised plasma — billions of tonnes — hurled off the Sun into space. If it reaches Earth, it drives the strongest space weather.">coronal mass ejection</Term>, a cloud of billions of tonnes of plasma flung into space. The largest of these are the most energetic events in the Solar System.

## We orbit inside the Sun's atmosphere

Even when it is quiet, the Sun steadily leaks charged particles in all directions — the <Term def="The constant stream of charged particles flowing out from the Sun in every direction, filling the Solar System out past the farthest planets.">solar wind</Term>. This wind blows a vast bubble around the whole Solar System, reaching far beyond Neptune. In a real sense, the planets do not orbit near the Sun so much as inside its outer atmosphere.

<FunFact>
The Sun's outer atmosphere, the corona, reaches one to three million degrees — hundreds of times hotter than the surface beneath it. Heat should not flow from the cooler surface to the hotter corona, and exactly how the corona gets so hot remains one of the great unsolved problems in solar physics.
</FunFact>

## Space weather and the aurora

When a coronal mass ejection sweeps past Earth, our planet's magnetic field catches the incoming particles and funnels them toward the poles. There they slam into the upper atmosphere and make it glow — the shimmering curtains of the aurora, the northern and southern lights.

<Term def="The glowing light in the sky near the poles, produced when charged particles from the Sun strike gas high in Earth's atmosphere and make it glow.">Aurorae</Term> are the gentle face of space weather. The violent face is the disruption a big storm brings: it can knock out satellites, scramble radio and GPS, and overload power grids on the ground.

<FunFact>
In 1859 the strongest solar storm on record, the Carrington Event, lit up aurorae bright enough to read by as far south as the tropics — and made telegraph lines spark, shocking operators and setting paper alight. A storm that size today, striking a world that runs on electronics, could cause damage measured in trillions.
</FunFact>

<KeyIdea>
The Sun is a variable, magnetic star, not a fixed lamp. Its tangled magnetic field drives sunspots, flares, and the solar wind, and that activity is a kind of weather that fills the Solar System — space weather. Earth sits inside it, which is why events on the Sun can light our skies and disrupt our machines.
</KeyIdea>

The Sun's reach does not end with its light and warmth. Its gravity holds, and its wind bathes, a whole family of worlds. That family — the Solar System — is where we go next.
