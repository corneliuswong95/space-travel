The strangest discoveries in astronomy are not distant objects but facts about space and time themselves. Almost all of them flow from one quiet rule we have leaned on throughout this course: light travels at a fixed speed — fast, but not infinite. Follow that single fact to its conclusions, and the universe stops behaving the way everyday life suggests it should.

## Light is fast, but not instant

Light crosses space at almost exactly 300,000 kilometres every second — quick enough to lap the Earth seven times while you read this sentence. Nothing we know of moves faster. Yet the universe is so large that even this speed can feel slow: light takes 1.3 seconds to reach us from the Moon, eight minutes from the Sun, and over four years from the next nearest star.

## The light-year is a time machine

Because light takes time to arrive, looking out into space is always looking back into the past. A <Term def="The distance light travels in one year, about 9.5 trillion kilometres. Because light takes a year to cross it, an object one light-year away is seen as it was a year ago.">light-year</Term> measures distance, but it also measures delay.

<KeyIdea>
Every telescope is a time machine. The Sun you see is eight minutes old; the nearest stars, years old; distant galaxies, billions of years old. We can never see anything in the universe as it is right now — only as it once was. To look far out is to look far back, and the deepest images we have show the universe as it was when it was young.
</KeyIdea>

## Nothing outruns light

The speed of light is not merely how fast light happens to travel — it is the universe's absolute speed limit. Nothing carrying matter or information can reach it, let alone beat it. This is why the science-fiction dream of simply flying faster hits a wall: as you push something with mass closer to light speed, it takes ever more energy, and reaching the limit would take an infinite amount. The barrier is built into the fabric of reality, not into our engines.

## Time is not the same for everyone

Einstein's first great shock was that time itself is not fixed. The faster you move, the slower your clock runs compared with someone standing still — an effect called <Term def="The slowing of time for something that is moving quickly, or sitting deep in a gravitational field, relative to an observer who is not. It is tiny at everyday speeds but real and measured.">time dilation</Term>. At everyday speeds the effect is far too small to feel, but it is real, and it is measured constantly.

<FunFact>
Your phone's navigation depends on Einstein being right. The satellites in the GPS network move fast and sit higher in Earth's gravity, so their clocks tick at a slightly different rate from clocks on the ground. Left uncorrected, the mismatch would throw your position off by about 10 kilometres a day.
</FunFact>

## Gravity bends space and time

Einstein's deeper idea was that gravity is not a force reaching across space but a bending of space and time by mass. A heavy object curves the space and time around it, and other objects simply follow the straightest available path through that curve — which is why planets orbit the Sun. Even light, which has no mass, follows the curve: starlight passing a massive object is deflected, an effect called <Term def="The bending of light by gravity as it passes a massive object, which can magnify or distort the image of whatever lies behind. It confirmed Einstein's theory and is now a working tool of astronomy.">gravitational lensing</Term>.

This was the test that made Einstein famous. During a total solar eclipse in 1919, astronomers measured starlight bending as it grazed the Sun — by exactly the amount his theory predicted.

<FunFact>
Astronomers now use gravity itself as a lens. A massive galaxy in the foreground can bend and magnify the light of something far behind it, acting as a natural telescope and revealing objects otherwise much too faint and distant to detect.
</FunFact>

## Reading the future, too

Light lets us read the universe's distant past directly. And the same physics — gravity shaping space and time, the expansion carrying the galaxies apart — lets us turn the question around and ask what lies ahead. If the cosmos had a beginning, does it also have an end? That is the final question of the course.
