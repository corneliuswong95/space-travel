The search for life beyond Earth may not point to a planet at all. Some of the Solar System's icy moons hide oceans of liquid water beneath their frozen shells — and a few may hold more water than all of Earth's seas. Far from the Sun, in the cold outer system, these are among the most promising places we know of to look for life.

## Liquid water without sunlight

Out by the giant planets, sunlight is far too weak to keep a surface warm, and everything freezes. So how can these moons hold liquid oceans? The answer is <Term def="Heating produced inside a moon as a giant planet's gravity repeatedly stretches and squeezes it. This internal friction can keep an ocean liquid far from the Sun's warmth.">tidal heating</Term>. As a moon orbits its massive planet, that planet's gravity flexes it like a squeezed ball, and the friction warms its interior — enough to keep a hidden ocean liquid under the ice.

## Europa: an ocean under ice

Jupiter's moon Europa is wrapped in a cracked shell of ice, and beneath it lies a salty <Term def="An ocean of liquid water beneath a moon's frozen surface, kept liquid by internal heat rather than sunlight.">subsurface ocean</Term>, flexed and warmed by Jupiter's gravity. NASA's Europa Clipper, launched in 2024, is on its way to survey it.

<FunFact>
Europa is smaller than our own Moon, yet its buried ocean may hold roughly twice as much liquid water as all of Earth's oceans put together.
</FunFact>

## Enceladus: a moon that sprays its ocean

Saturn's small moon Enceladus does something remarkable: from cracks at its south pole, it jets fountains of water vapour and ice grains straight into space. The Cassini spacecraft flew directly through these <Term def="A jet of water vapour and ice sprayed from cracks in a moon's surface, fed by the ocean beneath. Enceladus's plumes let a spacecraft sample its hidden ocean from orbit.">plumes</Term> and analysed them.

<FunFact>
By flying through the plumes of Enceladus, Cassini effectively tasted an alien ocean without ever landing — and found salts and organic molecules, the very ingredients that make an ocean promising for life.
</FunFact>

## Titan: seas of methane

Saturn's largest moon, Titan, is stranger still. It has a thick nitrogen atmosphere — denser than Earth's — and it is the only other place in the Solar System with stable liquid on its surface. But at −180 °C, those lakes, rivers, and seas are not water; they are liquid methane and ethane, fed by methane rain from an orange, smoggy sky. A water ocean may lie deeper down.

<KeyIdea>
Liquid water does not need a warm, sunlit surface. Tidal heating can keep oceans liquid billions of kilometres from the Sun, sealed beneath ice. That dramatically widens where life might exist — turning a handful of icy moons into some of the best places in the Solar System to search, alongside Mars.
</KeyIdea>

For all these nearby wonders, most of the universe is far too distant to visit. Everything else we know, we have had to read from its light — beginning with the sky you can see for yourself on any clear night.
