A star is less an object than a process — a slow, billion-year balancing act between gravity pulling inward and fusion pushing out. That balance cannot last forever. Eventually the fuel runs low, gravity gains the upper hand, and the star dies. How dramatically it dies comes down almost entirely to one thing: its mass.

## Born in clouds of gas and dust

Stars form inside <Term def="A vast cloud of gas and dust in space. The cold, dense regions of a nebula are the nurseries where new stars are born.">nebulae</Term> — cold clouds drifting between the existing stars. Where a cloud is dense enough, gravity pulls it inward, and as the gas falls together it heats up. When the centre grows hot and dense enough — around 10 million °C — fusion ignites, and a new star switches on. The same collapse that built the Sun left a leftover disc of material around it, which became the planets.

## The main sequence: a long, steady adulthood

Once fusion begins, a star settles into the longest phase of its life, fusing hydrogen into helium in its core. This stage is called the main sequence, and stars spend about 90% of their lives in it. The Sun is roughly halfway through its own ten-billion-year run.

Mass sets the pace, and not gently. A heavyweight star burns through its fuel recklessly and lasts only a few million years; the smallest <Term def="A small, cool, low-mass star. Red dwarfs are the most common stars in the galaxy and burn their fuel so slowly they can shine for trillions of years.">red dwarfs</Term> burn so slowly they will shine for trillions of years — far longer than the universe has yet existed.

<FunFact>
No red dwarf has ever died of old age. They live so long — many trillions of years — that the universe, at just 13.8 billion years old, is nowhere near old enough for even the very first of them to have run out of fuel.
</FunFact>

## When the core runs dry

When a Sun-like star finally exhausts the hydrogen in its core, the balance tips. The core contracts and heats, fusion spreads to a shell around it, and the outer layers swell enormously. The star becomes a <Term def="A dying star that has run low on core hydrogen and ballooned to many times its size, its surface cooling to a red glow.">red giant</Term>, large enough to engulf its nearest planets. In its final act, a star like the Sun gently puffs those outer layers off into space, leaving the exposed core behind: a <Term def="The dense, Earth-sized core left when a Sun-like star sheds its outer layers. It no longer fuses anything and slowly cools over billions of years.">white dwarf</Term>, about the size of Earth but holding much of the original star's mass.

## How the heavyweights die: supernova

Stars much heavier than the Sun end far more violently. They run hot enough to keep fusing — helium into carbon, carbon into heavier elements, on up to iron. But iron is the end of the line: fusing it absorbs energy instead of releasing it. With nothing left to hold it up, the core collapses in under a second, and the infalling outer layers rebound in a titanic explosion — a <Term def="The explosive death of a massive star, briefly outshining an entire galaxy and forging and scattering heavy elements across space.">supernova</Term> — that can briefly outshine all the billions of stars in its galaxy put together.

<KeyIdea>
Nearly every element heavier than helium — the oxygen you breathe, the calcium in your bones, the iron in your blood — was forged inside stars, with the heaviest made in their explosive deaths. Those stars then scattered the material into space, where it formed new stars, planets, and eventually us. We are, quite literally, made of star stuff.
</KeyIdea>

## What gets left behind

A supernova does not destroy everything. The crushed core can survive as one of the densest objects in the universe — a neutron star, or, for the heaviest stars, a black hole. Those extreme remnants are strange enough to deserve a lesson of their own, which is exactly where we head next.

<FunFact>
Betelgeuse, the bright reddish star marking Orion's shoulder, is a red supergiant near the end of its life. When it explodes — sometime in the next 100,000 years, which could mean tonight — it will be bright enough to see in broad daylight and to cast shadows at night.
</FunFact>

Mass writes the whole story. Lightweight stars fade quietly to white dwarfs; heavyweights detonate and seed the galaxy with the raw material of new worlds. Grasp that single rule and you understand most of how the night sky came to be.
