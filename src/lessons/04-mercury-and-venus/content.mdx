Between Earth and the Sun lie two rocky worlds that could hardly be more different from each other. One is a cratered, airless cinder with the wildest temperatures in the Solar System; the other is a cloud-wrapped furnace, nearly Earth's twin in size but ruined by its own atmosphere. Together they teach a lesson that matters for every planet, including ours: distance from the Sun is not the whole story.

## Mercury: a cratered cinder

Mercury is the smallest planet and the closest to the Sun. It has almost no atmosphere — nothing to trap heat or carry it from the day side to the night side. The result is the most extreme temperature swing of any planet: around 430 °C in direct sun, plunging to about −180 °C in darkness.

With no air and no weather to wear them down, Mercury's craters have survived for billions of years, leaving it looking much like our Moon. Its spin is strange, too: it turns exactly three times for every two orbits, so a single day from one sunrise to the next lasts about 176 Earth days — longer than Mercury's 88-day year.

<FunFact>
The planet closest to the Sun has ice. Because Mercury barely tilts, the floors of some craters at its poles never see sunlight at all, and in that permanent shadow water ice has survived — a few metres from surfaces hot enough to melt lead.
</FunFact>

## Venus: Earth's twin gone wrong

Venus is almost exactly Earth's size and mass — often called our twin. There the resemblance ends. A thick atmosphere of carbon dioxide traps heat so effectively, in a <Term def="A greenhouse effect that feeds on itself: trapped heat drives more greenhouse gas into the air, which traps still more heat. Venus is the Solar System's clearest example.">runaway greenhouse effect</Term>, that its surface sits at about 464 °C — hotter than Mercury, even though Venus is nearly twice as far from the Sun.

The air itself is crushing: the pressure at the surface is about 90 times Earth's, the same as being 900 metres deep in the ocean. Above it all float clouds of sulfuric acid. And Venus turns backwards, so slowly that its day is longer than its year.

<FunFact>
On Venus, a day is longer than a year: it takes 243 Earth days to spin once but only 225 to orbit the Sun. And because it spins backwards, the Sun there rises in the west and sets in the east.
</FunFact>

## What both worlds teach

<KeyIdea>
Distance from the Sun is not destiny. Mercury, the closest planet, swings between scorching and frigid because it has almost no air to hold heat. Venus, farther out, is the hottest planet of all because its air traps heat so well. A planet's atmosphere can matter as much as its orbit — a sobering thing to keep in mind when we think about the climate of our own world.
</KeyIdea>

Between these two extremes — the airless cinder and the runaway furnace — sits a third rocky world that, against considerable odds, got the balance right. That world is ours.
