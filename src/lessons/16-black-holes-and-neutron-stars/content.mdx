When the most massive stars die, they leave behind objects that seem to break the rules. Matter is crushed so far that a sugar cube of it would outweigh a mountain — and in the most extreme case, gravity grows so strong that not even light can escape.

## Neutron stars: a sun crushed into a city

When a heavy star's core collapses in a supernova, gravity can force its protons and electrons to merge into neutrons, packed as tightly as the inside of an atom's nucleus. The result is a <Term def="The collapsed core left after a supernova: roughly 1.4 times the Sun's mass packed into a sphere only about 20 km across. A sugar-cube-sized piece would weigh about a billion tonnes.">neutron star</Term> — around 1.4 times the mass of the Sun squeezed into a ball the size of a city, perhaps 20 km across.

<Readout label="Neutron star · density" value="~1 billion" unit="tonnes/cm³" />

Many neutron stars spin astonishingly fast — some hundreds of times every second — flinging beams of radiation around like a lighthouse. When such a beam happens to sweep across Earth, we pick up a precise, rapid pulse and call the object a <Term def="A rapidly spinning neutron star that sweeps beams of radiation past Earth, which we detect as regular pulses — sometimes hundreds per second.">pulsar</Term>.

## When gravity wins outright: black holes

If the collapsing core is heavier still — above roughly three times the Sun's mass — nothing known can halt the collapse. The matter is crushed without limit, and the result is a <Term def="A region of space where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape from within a certain boundary.">black hole</Term>: a place where gravity has become so intense that not even light moves fast enough to climb back out.

## The event horizon: the point of no return

A black hole is not a solid object you could strike. What defines it is a boundary called the <Term def="The boundary around a black hole beyond which nothing can escape. It is not a surface but a line in space past which the escape speed exceeds the speed of light.">event horizon</Term> — the line past which the speed needed to escape exceeds the speed of light. Cross it, and there is no return; stay outside, and you feel only ordinary gravity.

<FunFact>
Black holes are not cosmic vacuum cleaners. If the Sun were swapped for a black hole of exactly the same mass, Earth's orbit would not change in the slightest — we would freeze in the dark, but keep circling just as before. Gravity depends on mass and distance, not on whether something is a black hole.
</FunFact>

## From stellar to supermassive

The black holes left by dying stars hold a few to a few dozen times the Sun's mass. But there is a second, far larger class: <Term def="A black hole millions to billions of times the mass of the Sun, found at the centre of most large galaxies, including our own.">supermassive black holes</Term>, weighing millions or billions of suns, that sit at the centres of galaxies. Our own Milky Way has one — Sagittarius A\*, about four million times the mass of the Sun — anchoring its heart.

## How we see the invisible

If light cannot escape a black hole, how do we know they are there? By their effects. We watch stars whip around an unseen point at the galaxy's centre. We see gas heated to millions of degrees as it spirals inward, blazing in X-rays just before it crosses the horizon. In 2015 we began detecting <Term def="Ripples in space and time, predicted by Einstein, produced when very massive objects accelerate — such as two black holes spiralling together and merging.">gravitational waves</Term> — literal ripples in space — from black holes colliding. And in 2019, astronomers linked telescopes across the planet to capture the first image of a black hole's silhouette.

<FunFact>
That first image, of the black hole in the galaxy M87, shows a dark shadow tens of billions of kilometres across — larger than our entire Solar System. The black hole is so far away that photographing it was compared to reading the date on a coin in a distant city.
</FunFact>

<KeyIdea>
Neutron stars and black holes are what gravity produces when nothing is left to resist it. They are invisible, or nearly so, yet we map them precisely by their pull on everything around them — the clearest proof that the most powerful objects in the universe can be studied without ever being seen directly.
</KeyIdea>

We have now followed stars from their birth in cold clouds to their steady lives and their strange, dense remains. But stars are rarely alone — most are circled by planets. Having seen what the stars themselves are, we turn to the worlds that orbit them.
