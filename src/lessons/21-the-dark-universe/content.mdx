Everything this course has described — the Sun, the planets, the stars, the galaxies, and you — is made of ordinary matter. Add it all up, and it comes to less than 5% of the universe. The other 95% is two things we have named but genuinely cannot explain. It is the largest gap in all of physics.

## The visible universe is the minority

Stars, gas, planets, and people are all built from atoms, and atoms account for under a twentieth of everything there is. Roughly a quarter of the universe is <Term def="An unseen form of matter that gives off no light but has gravity. It outweighs ordinary matter by about five to one and holds galaxies together.">dark matter</Term>, and the largest share — about 68% — is <Term def="A mysterious form of energy that fills empty space and pushes the expansion of the universe to speed up. Its nature is unknown.">dark energy</Term>. Neither gives off light; neither is made of atoms.

## Dark matter: the missing glue

The first clue came from spinning galaxies. They rotate so fast that the gravity of everything we can see is nowhere near enough to hold them together — by rights they should fling their stars into space. Some unseen mass must be supplying the extra gravity. The same hidden mass shows up in how galaxy clusters move, in the way gravity bends light around them, and in the afterglow of the Big Bang. We have ruled out ordinary explanations like faint gas or black holes; dark matter appears to be some entirely new kind of particle that simply ignores light.

<FunFact>
Dark matter is streaming through you right now, and through the whole Earth, without leaving a trace. Physicists have built ultra-sensitive detectors deep underground, shielded from all other interference, hoping to catch a single particle of it — and so far have caught nothing.
</FunFact>

## Dark energy: the cosmic accelerator

The second surprise came in 1998. Astronomers measuring distant exploding stars expected to find the universe's expansion gradually slowing under gravity. Instead they found the opposite: the expansion is speeding up. Something is pushing space apart ever faster — a force that seems to be a property of empty space itself. We call it dark energy, and we do not know what it is.

<FunFact>
The discovery that the expansion is accelerating stunned astronomers — nobody had predicted it — and it earned the 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics. Overnight, most of the universe became something with no name and no explanation.
</FunFact>

<KeyIdea>
Two independent lines of evidence — galaxies that spin too fast to hold together, and an expansion that is speeding up — force the same strange conclusion: about 95% of the universe is invisible and unexplained. Dark matter's gravity built the scaffolding on which galaxies grew; dark energy will decide how the whole story ends. Naming them is not the same as understanding them.
</KeyIdea>

To even talk about expanding space, accelerating distances, and gravity that bends light, we have been quietly leaning on deeper ideas about space and time themselves. It is time to look at those directly.
