For all of history, we could only look up. In the span of a single lifetime that changed: we built machines that leave the ground, and sent them — and, a few times, ourselves — out to the worlds this course describes. Getting off the Earth turns out to be less about power than about speed, and the physics behind it is stranger and simpler than it looks.

## What an orbit really is

An orbit is not a way of escaping gravity. It is a way of falling. Isaac Newton imagined firing a cannonball horizontally, faster and faster: eventually it would fly so fast that, as it fell, the ground curved away beneath it just as quickly — and it would fall forever, all the way around the planet. That is an <Term def="A path in which an object falls continuously around a planet: it moves sideways so fast that it keeps missing the ground. Not an absence of gravity, but a permanent state of falling.">orbit</Term>. A satellite is not floating free of gravity; it is falling, endlessly, moving sideways fast enough to keep missing the Earth.

<FunFact>
Astronauts on the Space Station float not because gravity is absent — there is nearly as much up there as on the ground — but because they and their station are falling together, in continuous free fall. Circling the Earth every 90 minutes, the crew sees about 16 sunrises and sunsets a day.
</FunFact>

## Why rockets are almost all fuel

To reach orbit you must get moving at some 28,000 km/h; to leave Earth's gravity entirely takes the <Term def="The speed something needs to break free of a planet's gravity for good. For Earth it is about 11.2 km/s, or 40,000 km/h.">escape velocity</Term> of about 11.2 km/s. A rocket has to carry all its own propellant and hurl it downward to push itself up. This traps it in a cruel arithmetic: more fuel means more weight to lift, which needs still more fuel. That is why rockets are overwhelmingly fuel by mass, and why they shed empty stages as they climb, dropping dead weight along the way.

## Robots go first

Almost everywhere we have explored, we sent machines. Flybys, orbiters, landers, and rovers have now visited every planet in the Solar System. Robots can go where people cannot: they need no air or return trip, cost far less, and can work for years or decades in places that would kill a human in minutes.

## Borrowing a planet's motion

Deep-space probes get help from an elegant trick called a <Term def="A manoeuvre in which a spacecraft swings close past a planet and borrows a little of the planet's motion around the Sun, gaining speed without using fuel.">gravity assist</Term>: by swinging close past a planet, a probe steals a sliver of that planet's orbital motion and flings itself onward, faster, without burning fuel. Voyager toured the outer planets this way, hopping from one to the next.

<FunFact>
Voyager 1, launched in 1977, is the most distant human-made object — over 25 billion kilometres away and now drifting through interstellar space, still faintly calling home. It carries a golden record of Earth's sounds and images, in case anything, someday, finds it.
</FunFact>

## Sending people is the hard part

Humans are demanding cargo. We need air, water, food, warmth, shielding from radiation, and a way home. We have walked on the Moon and we live continuously aboard the Space Station in low orbit, but sending people deeper — to Mars and beyond — means long journeys, heavy supplies, and dangerous radiation. That is why robots have gone almost everywhere first.

<KeyIdea>
Orbiting is not escaping gravity; it is falling sideways so fast that you keep missing the planet. Once that clicks, spaceflight stops looking like magic: rockets are machines for reaching the necessary speed, robots do the far-flung exploring, and a planet's own gravity can be borrowed for a free boost.
</KeyIdea>

Rockets have carried us and our machines across the Solar System. But the stars stay impossibly far — light-years, not light-hours, away. To understand them we return to light and careful reasoning, beginning with the question of where stars come from in the first place.
