Beyond Mars and the asteroid belt, the planets change character completely. There is no more ground to stand on. The outer four are giants — vast balls of gas and ice that together hold almost all of the planetary mass in the Solar System, and keep most of its moons.

## Gas giants and ice giants

The four are not all alike. Jupiter and Saturn are <Term def="A planet made mostly of hydrogen and helium — the same elements as the Sun — with no solid surface. Jupiter and Saturn are the Solar System's two gas giants.">gas giants</Term>, built almost entirely of hydrogen and helium, the lightest elements. Uranus and Neptune are smaller, colder <Term def="A planet made largely of heavier compounds — water, ammonia, and methane — which astronomers loosely call ices. Uranus and Neptune are the Solar System's two ice giants.">ice giants</Term>, with far more water, ammonia, and methane mixed in. All four likely hide a small core, buried under thousands of kilometres of crushing atmosphere.

## Jupiter, the giant among giants

Jupiter is the largest planet by an enormous margin — more than twice the mass of all the other planets combined.

Its most famous feature is the <Term def="A colossal storm in Jupiter's atmosphere, wider than the whole Earth, that has been observed for at least 350 years.">Great Red Spot</Term>, a storm broader than our entire planet that has been turning for centuries. Jupiter also spins faster than any other planet, completing a full day in under ten hours despite its size.

<FunFact>
Jupiter is sometimes called a failed star. It is made of the same hydrogen and helium as the Sun, but to ignite fusion in its core it would need to be about 80 times more massive than it is. It fell far short — so instead of a second sun, we got a planet.
</FunFact>

## Saturn and its rings

Saturn carries the brightest rings in the Solar System — not solid bands but billions of separate pieces of ice and rock, ranging from grains to boulders, each on its own orbit. The rings span a width greater than the Earth–Moon distance, yet are often only about ten metres thick. Scaled to a sheet of paper's width, they would be thousands of times thinner than the paper.

<FunFact>
Saturn is the least dense planet in the Solar System — for its size, lighter than water. If you could find an ocean big enough to hold it, Saturn would float.
</FunFact>

## Uranus on its side, Neptune in the dark

The ice giants are stranger still. Uranus is tipped almost completely over, with an <Term def="The angle of a planet's spin axis relative to its orbit. Earth's is 23°, which gives us seasons; Uranus is tipped about 98°, so it essentially rolls around the Sun.">axial tilt</Term> of about 98°. As it rolls along its orbit, each pole spends roughly 42 years in unbroken daylight, then 42 in darkness.

Neptune, the farthest planet, receives barely any sunlight — yet it has the fastest winds in the Solar System, reaching some 2,000 km/h. Both worlds glow deep blue: their atmospheres are rich in methane, which soaks up red light and scatters blue back into space.

<FunFact>
Deep inside Uranus and Neptune, it may rain diamonds. Under the crushing pressure, methane is thought to break apart and its carbon to crystallise, so that showers of solid diamond drift slowly down toward each planet's core.
</FunFact>

## The real prizes are the moons

For all the grandeur of the planets themselves, some of the most interesting places out here are their moons. Jupiter's Europa and Saturn's Enceladus both hide oceans of liquid water beneath shells of ice — Enceladus even jets it into space through cracks at its south pole. Saturn's Titan has a thick atmosphere and lakes of liquid methane. Jupiter's Io is the most volcanic body known. Between them, the giants hold the Solar System's best chances of finding life beyond Earth.

<KeyIdea>
The outer planets are not just larger versions of the inner ones — they are a different kind of world, with no surface, deep atmospheres, ring systems, and dozens of moons apiece. Several of those moons, warmed from within and holding liquid water, are now leading targets in the search for life.
</KeyIdea>

The giants are the last of the true planets. But the Solar System does not stop with them — it is littered with billions of smaller bodies, the rubble left over from its birth.
