For most of human history, the night sky was a calendar, a compass, and a map. You don't need a telescope to use it — only a way to name what you are looking at and to find it again. Those are the same tools printed in the margins of every star atlas.

## Constellations are patterns, not places

A <Term def="One of 88 official regions of the sky, named for the pattern its brightest stars trace. The stars in a constellation usually have nothing to do with one another — they only line up from our viewpoint.">constellation</Term> is a pattern the eye joins together — a hunter, a swan, a bear. But the stars in any constellation are usually nowhere near each other in space. They can lie tens or hundreds of light-years apart, aligned only by chance along our line of sight. Astronomers now use the 88 constellations to divide the whole sky into named regions, like countries on a map.

<Aside>
Some famous shapes are not constellations at all. The Plough, or Big Dipper, is an <Term def="A recognisable pattern of stars that is not one of the 88 official constellations — often a piece of a larger one.">asterism</Term>: a pattern within the larger constellation of Ursa Major, the Great Bear.
</Aside>

## Magnitude: how bright a star looks

Stargazers measure brightness on a scale of <Term def="A measure of how bright a star looks from Earth. The scale runs backwards: smaller numbers are brighter, and the brightest objects have negative values.">apparent magnitude</Term> — and it runs backwards. The brighter an object, the lower its number. Sirius, the brightest night-time star, sits at magnitude −1.5; the faintest stars a sharp eye can catch on a dark night are around +6.

<Readout label="Sirius · apparent magnitude" value="−1.46" />

Each step of one magnitude is about 2.5 times the brightness, so a first-magnitude star is roughly a hundred times brighter than a sixth-magnitude one.

## Bright because it's close, or bright because it's fierce?

A star can look brilliant for two completely different reasons: it is genuinely luminous, or it is simply nearby. Apparent magnitude cannot tell them apart. To compare stars fairly, astronomers also use <Term def="How bright a star would appear if every star were placed at the same standard distance. It measures a star's true light output, stripping away the effect of distance.">absolute magnitude</Term> — the brightness a star would show from a fixed distance.

The difference is enormous. Sirius looks like the brightest star in our sky mostly because it is close, just 8.6 light-years away. Rigel, in Orion, looks fainter only because it is far — yet it pours out several thousand times more light than Sirius. The night sky mixes nearby embers with distant furnaces, and the eye cannot sort them by looking alone.

## Coordinates: finding anything in the sky

To point someone to an object, astronomers treat the sky as a giant sphere and give every point an address, much like latitude and longitude on Earth. <Term def="The sky's coordinate system. Right ascension (RA) runs like longitude, measured in hours; declination (Dec) runs like latitude, measured in degrees.">Right ascension and declination</Term> pin down any star, galaxy, or planet.

<Readout label="Sirius · RA / Dec" value="06h 45m / −16° 43′" />

Give those two numbers, a date, and a time, and anyone with a star chart can swing straight to the same spot.

## The sky turns, and drifts

Two motions change what you see. Earth's daily spin sweeps the whole sky from east to west each night, wheeling around the point above the pole — marked, for now, by Polaris. And Earth's year-long orbit means we face a different direction in space each season, so the constellations of winter are not the ones of summer.

<FunFact>
Polaris has not always been the North Star, and will not stay one. Earth's axis slowly wobbles like a spinning top, a 26,000-year circle called precession. In about 12,000 years the bright star Vega will take a turn as our pole star — and the ancient Egyptians, building the pyramids, pointed them at a different pole star entirely.
</FunFact>

## Looking out is looking back

Every distance in the sky is also a delay. Light travels fast but not instantly, so a <Term def="The distance light travels in one year — about 9.5 trillion kilometres. Because light takes time to cross it, seeing something one light-year away means seeing it as it was one year ago.">light-year</Term> is both a distance and a glimpse into the past. The Sun you see is eight minutes old. The stars of a constellation are years to centuries old, each frozen at a different moment. You never see the sky as it is — only as it was.

<FunFact>
The farthest thing most people can see with the naked eye is the Andromeda Galaxy, a faint smudge 2.5 million light-years away. The light landing in your eye left it 2.5 million years ago — before our species existed. To glance at it is to look further back in time than all of human history.
</FunFact>

<KeyIdea>
A constellation tells you where to look, magnitude tells you how bright it will be, and coordinates pin it exactly. Add a date and time, and that is enough to find any object in the sky — which is all a star atlas really is: a record of what is up there, and where.
</KeyIdea>

That is the whole craft of reading the sky with the naked eye: not memorising thousands of stars, but knowing the few tools that let you find any of them. To see further, though — and to learn what those points of light actually are — we need to gather more light than the eye can. That is the work of the telescope.
