Almost everything in this course arrived as light. We have never touched a star, scooped up a galaxy, or visited another sun. We decoded them from the faint light they sent across space — and light, it turns out, carries far more than simple brightness. Telescopes are the instruments that catch it, and reading it is the heart of astronomy.

## Telescopes are buckets for light

A telescope's first job is to gather light. A larger mirror collects more of it, so it can reveal fainter, more distant objects and finer detail — which is why observatories are locked in a race to build ever-bigger mirrors. The largest space telescope yet, JWST, carries a mirror 6.5 metres across, built from gold-coated segments that unfolded in space.

## The whole spectrum, not just visible light

The light our eyes see is a thin slice of the full <Term def="The full range of light, from radio waves through microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, and X-rays to gamma rays. Visible light is only a narrow band of it.">electromagnetic spectrum</Term>. Objects in space shine across all of it: cool gas and dust glow in infrared and radio; violent, superheated matter blazes in X-rays and gamma rays. To see the whole universe, astronomers build different telescopes for different kinds of light. Infrared telescopes like JWST can even see through clouds of dust that block visible light, and peer back to the universe's first galaxies.

## Why some telescopes must leave Earth

Our atmosphere is both a shield and a nuisance. It smears starlight into a twinkle, and it absorbs most ultraviolet, X-ray, and much infrared light before it reaches the ground. Putting a telescope in space, above the air, gives sharper images and opens up the wavelengths the atmosphere blocks. That is why Hubble, launched in 1990, and JWST after it, were sent to orbit rather than built on a mountaintop.

## Splitting light: the cosmic barcode

The most powerful trick of all is <Term def="Splitting light into its component colours to read the pattern of bright and dark lines within it. Because each element makes its own pattern, a spectrum reveals what something is made of, how hot it is, and how fast it is moving.">spectroscopy</Term>. Spread a beam of starlight into its colours and a pattern of sharp lines appears — a barcode. Each chemical element imprints its own set of lines, so the spectrum reveals what a distant object is made of, how hot it is, and — from how far the lines are shifted — how fast it is moving toward or away from us.

<FunFact>
Helium was discovered on the Sun before anyone found it on Earth. In 1868 astronomers spotted a spectral line in sunlight that matched no known element, and named the new element after Helios, the Greek sun god. It was identified on Earth only decades later.
</FunFact>

<KeyIdea>
Light is the universal messenger. Its brightness hints at an object's distance and power; its spectrum reveals composition, temperature, and motion. Almost everything we know about the universe beyond the Solar System was read from light we caught and split apart — which is why every leap in telescope-building rewrites the textbooks.
</KeyIdea>

<FunFact>
We have never brought home a piece of a star. Every fact about the stars — what they are made of, how hot they burn, how fast they move, how old they are — has been decoded from their light alone.
</FunFact>

Catching light is one way to reach the universe. In the span of a single lifetime we found another: leaving the ground entirely and going out to meet it.
