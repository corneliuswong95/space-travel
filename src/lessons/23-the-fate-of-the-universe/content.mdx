We have followed the universe forward from a hot, dense birth 13.8 billion years ago, through the first stars, the galaxies, and the space stretching between them. One question is left, and it is the largest of all: how does it end?

## The ending hinges on dark energy

The universe's fate comes down to a contest between two forces: gravity, pulling everything together, and dark energy, pushing space apart. Everything we can measure says dark energy is winning, and the expansion is speeding up. If that continues, the universe expands forever.

## The long, cold fade

An expansion without end leads somewhere quiet. Distant galaxies are carried beyond the reach of our telescopes; the gas that makes stars runs low and star formation ceases; the existing stars burn out one by one; and the universe drifts toward a cold, dark, near-empty state. Astronomers call this the <Term def="The likely far-future state of the universe: expanded, cooled, and run down, with the stars burned out and energy spread too thinly to do anything. Also called the Big Freeze.">heat death</Term>, or the Big Freeze.

## The other ways it might go

There are alternatives. If dark energy grew stronger over time, it could eventually tear apart galaxies, stars, and atoms themselves — a "Big Rip." If it reversed and gravity took over, the expansion could go into reverse and collapse in a "Big Crunch." But what we actually measure points to neither of these: the slow freeze is the front-runner.

## Timescales beyond imagining

The far future unfolds over spans that dwarf the present. Star formation winds down over trillions of years; the last dim stars fade long after that; and even black holes are not permanent — they slowly leak away through a faint glow called <Term def="A faint radiation predicted by Stephen Hawking that causes black holes to slowly lose mass and, over almost unimaginable spans of time, eventually evaporate.">Hawking radiation</Term>, taking so long to evaporate that the current age of the universe is less than a rounding error beside it.

<FunFact>
We live cosmically early. In the far future, the accelerating expansion will have swept every other galaxy out of view, leaving any astronomers of that era with an empty sky — and perhaps no way to ever discover the Big Bang. We are lucky enough to live while the evidence is still overhead.
</FunFact>

<KeyIdea>
The likeliest ending is not a bang but a slow fade: expansion carrying everything apart, the stars burning out, the universe cooling toward darkness over timescales that make its present age look instantaneous. We live in the cosmos's brief, brilliant morning — when the sky is still full of stars, and other galaxies still hang within reach of our telescopes.
</KeyIdea>
