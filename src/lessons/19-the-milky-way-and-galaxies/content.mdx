Every star you have ever seen with your own eyes belongs to a single galaxy — ours. Step back far enough and the Sun is not the centre of anything: it is one ordinary point among a few hundred billion, drifting through the suburbs of a vast spiral.

## What a galaxy is

A <Term def="A huge system of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter bound together by gravity. Galaxies are the basic building blocks of the universe on the largest scales.">galaxy</Term> is an enormous collection of stars — along with gas, dust, and dark matter — all held together by gravity. Our galaxy, the Milky Way, contains somewhere between 100 and 400 billion stars. It is so wide that light, the fastest thing there is, takes about 100,000 years just to cross it.

<Aside>
Galaxies spin so fast that their visible stars and gas ought to fly apart — there is not enough matter we can see to hold them together. The missing gravity is credited to dark matter, an unseen substance that seems to outweigh everything visible by about five to one. We have never detected it directly; we only see its pull.
</Aside>

## Our address in the Milky Way

The Milky Way is a barred spiral: a flat disc of stars wound into great arms, with a bar of stars across the middle. The Sun sits a little over halfway out from the centre, on a minor spiral arm, some 26,000 light-years from the core. We are not at the centre, and there is nothing special about our spot.

The faint band of light that gives the galaxy its name is simply our own galaxy seen from the inside. Looking along the plane of the disc, we see the merged glow of countless distant stars run together into a river of light across the sky.

<FunFact>
The Sun takes about 230 million years to circle the centre of the galaxy once — a span sometimes called a galactic year. The last time the Sun was in its current position, the earliest dinosaurs were only just appearing. In its whole life so far, the Sun has completed only about twenty of these laps.
</FunFact>

## The heart of the galaxy

At the very centre of the Milky Way lies a supermassive black hole, Sagittarius A\*, with the mass of about four million Suns. We cannot see it directly, but we have watched stars near the core whip around an invisible point at thousands of kilometres per second — the unmistakable signature of an enormous, compact mass.

## Galaxies come in shapes

The Milky Way is one of many forms a galaxy can take. Spirals, like ours, have sweeping arms and ongoing star formation. Giant ellipticals are smooth, rounded swarms of older stars. Smaller, less orderly galaxies are simply called irregular. Galaxies are not scattered evenly through space, either — they gather into groups and clusters, themselves strung along vast filaments in a pattern called the cosmic web.

## On a collision course with Andromeda

Our nearest large neighbour, the Andromeda Galaxy, lies 2.5 million light-years away — and it is heading toward us. In roughly four and a half billion years the two galaxies will merge into one.

<FunFact>
When the Milky Way and Andromeda collide, almost no two stars will actually strike each other. Galaxies are so overwhelmingly empty that the stars will simply glide past, the two great cities of stars passing through one another with room to spare.
</FunFact>

## How many galaxies are there?

When astronomers aimed the Hubble Space Telescope at a patch of sky that looked utterly empty — about the size of a grain of sand held at arm's length — and stared for days, the darkness filled with thousands of galaxies, each a city of billions of stars. Scaled up to the whole sky, that result implies the observable universe holds something like two trillion galaxies.

<KeyIdea>
The Sun is one of a few hundred billion stars in the Milky Way, and the Milky Way is one of perhaps two trillion galaxies. Almost every point of light in those deep images is not a star at all, but an entire galaxy. Our whole night sky is a single galaxy's worth of a vastly larger universe.
</KeyIdea>

If there are two trillion galaxies all rushing apart, a natural question follows: rushing apart from what, and toward what? That is the story of the expanding universe.
