Are we alone? It is the oldest question we can ask about the sky, and for most of history it belonged to philosophers and storytellers. In the last few decades it has quietly become a science — with real instruments, real targets, and real ways to be proven wrong.

## What life needs

We only have one example of life — Earth's — so we start from what it requires: liquid water, a source of energy, and the right chemistry, built on carbon. Rather than guess at alien biology, scientists look for those conditions, and for the traces life leaves behind.

## Life is tougher than we thought

On Earth, life turns up where it has no business surviving: in boiling volcanic vents on the sea floor, in acid, in near-frozen brine, deep inside solid rock, even amid radiation that would kill us. These <Term def="An organism that thrives in conditions once thought impossible for life — extreme heat, cold, acidity, pressure, or radiation.">extremophiles</Term> show that life is far hardier and more adaptable than anyone once assumed, which widens the range of places it might survive elsewhere.

## Where we look

Close to home, the search focuses on Mars, which was once warm and wet, and on the ocean moons Europa and Enceladus, with their hidden seas. Far beyond, the targets are exoplanets — and there, we cannot land or sample, so we read the light.

## Reading life in light

When an exoplanet passes in front of its star, a little starlight filters through its atmosphere and picks up that atmosphere's spectral fingerprint. Certain combinations of gases — oxygen together with methane, for instance — are hard to keep up without something constantly replenishing them, so they would be a possible <Term def="A sign that could reveal life from afar — such as a combination of atmospheric gases that is difficult to sustain without living things constantly producing it.">biosignature</Term>. Telescopes like JWST have begun sniffing exoplanet atmospheres for exactly this.

## Listening, and framing the question

A different approach simply listens. <Term def="The Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence: scanning the sky for radio or light signals that might come from a technological civilisation.">SETI</Term> scans the sky for radio or light signals that no natural process would produce. Decades of listening have turned up nothing conclusive — though one unexplained 1977 signal, the "Wow! signal", has never been repeated or accounted for.

<Aside>
To organise such an uncertain question, astronomers use the Drake equation — not a formula that spits out an answer, but a way to break "how many civilisations could we contact?" into pieces we can try to estimate. It turns a daydream into a list of measurable unknowns.
</Aside>

<FunFact>
We have not only listened but called out. The Arecibo telescope beamed a coded message toward a distant star cluster in 1974, and the two Voyager probes carry golden records of Earth's sounds and images into interstellar space — messages in bottles, cast into the cosmic ocean.
</FunFact>

<KeyIdea>
We have found no life beyond Earth yet. But the question is now a genuine science: we sample the oceans of icy moons, read exoplanet atmospheres for biosignatures, and listen for signals. Even a firm silence would tell us something profound about how rare we are.
</KeyIdea>

Whether or not we have company, our search plays out on a stage of staggering size. To take its true measure we pull back — past the stars, to the galaxy that holds them all, and the universe beyond.
