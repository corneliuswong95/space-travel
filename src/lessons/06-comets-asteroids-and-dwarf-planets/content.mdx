The eight planets are the headline, but they are only a fraction of the Solar System's membership. Scattered between and beyond them are billions of smaller bodies — asteroids, comets, and dwarf planets — the rubble left over from the planets' formation, and some of the best-preserved samples of the early Solar System.

## Asteroids: the rocky leftovers

Most asteroids circle the Sun in the <Term def="A region between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter where most asteroids orbit. Jupiter's gravity kept the material there from ever gathering into a planet.">asteroid belt</Term>, between Mars and Jupiter. They are chunks of rock and metal that never managed to combine into a planet — Jupiter's gravity kept stirring the material up. They range in size from Ceres, nearly 1,000 km across and round enough to count as a dwarf planet too, down to lumps the size of a house.

<FunFact>
The asteroid belt is almost entirely empty space. Its millions of asteroids add up to less mass than our Moon, spread so thinly that spacecraft fly straight through without any need to dodge. The crowded, tumbling asteroid fields of films simply do not exist.
</FunFact>

## Comets: dirty snowballs

A <Term def="A small body of ice, dust, and rock from the outer Solar System. When its orbit brings it near the Sun, the ice turns to gas and forms a glowing head and one or more tails.">comet</Term> is mostly ice and dust — a dirty snowball left over from the cold outer Solar System. For most of its orbit it is a dark, frozen lump. But when its path carries it close to the Sun, the ice turns straight to gas, releasing dust and forming a glowing head and a tail that can stretch for millions of kilometres.

Comets fall in from two reservoirs far past the planets: the <Term def="A ring of icy bodies just beyond Neptune, the source of many shorter-period comets. Pluto is the largest known member.">Kuiper Belt</Term> just outside Neptune, and the <Term def="A vast spherical shell of icy bodies surrounding the Solar System, perhaps a quarter of the way to the nearest star. It is thought to be the source of long-period comets.">Oort cloud</Term> far beyond it.

<FunFact>
A comet's tail does not stream out behind it like smoke from a train. It is blown outward by sunlight and the solar wind, so it always points away from the Sun — which means that on the journey back out, a comet travels tail-first, effectively chasing its own tail.
</FunFact>

## Meteoroid, meteor, meteorite

Three similar words describe a single object at three stages:

<Aside>
A meteoroid is a small rock drifting through space. When it hits Earth's atmosphere and burns up in a streak of light, that streak is a meteor — a shooting star. If any piece survives the fall and reaches the ground, that surviving fragment is a meteorite.
</Aside>

Most meteors are caused by specks no larger than a grain of sand, vaporising tens of kilometres up. A meteor shower happens when Earth ploughs through the dusty trail a comet has left along its orbit, and dozens of these specks burn up every hour.

## Dwarf planets, and what happened to Pluto

Pluto was counted as the ninth planet from its discovery in 1930 until 2006, when astronomers settled on a formal definition of "planet" for the first time. To qualify, a body must orbit the Sun, be round, and have <Term def="To have become gravitationally dominant in its orbit, sweeping up or flinging away other bodies of comparable size. Pluto shares its zone with many icy bodies, so it fails this last test.">cleared its orbital neighbourhood</Term> of other objects. Pluto meets the first two conditions but not the third — it shares the Kuiper Belt with countless icy bodies — so it was reclassified as a <Term def="A body that orbits the Sun and is round, but has not cleared its orbit of other objects. Pluto, Eris, Ceres, Makemake, and Haumea are the recognised examples.">dwarf planet</Term>.

It was less a demotion than a recognition: Pluto turned out to be one of the largest members of a whole population of worlds out beyond Neptune.

<FunFact>
When NASA's New Horizons probe flew past Pluto in 2015 — after a nine-year journey — it revealed a surprisingly active world, with mountains of water ice and a vast, smooth glacier of frozen nitrogen shaped, entirely by chance, like a heart.
</FunFact>

## When they cross our path

These small bodies are not only distant curiosities. About 66 million years ago, an asteroid roughly 10 km wide struck what is now Mexico and ended the age of the dinosaurs. Smaller impacts are far more common — in 1908 an object exploded above Tunguska in Siberia, flattening forest across an area larger than a major city.

That risk is why, in 2022, NASA's DART mission deliberately crashed a spacecraft into a small asteroid and measurably shifted its orbit — the first time humans have ever changed the path of another world. The meteorites that do land are studied closely: they are free samples of space, and many are older than Earth itself.

<KeyIdea>
The planets grew from countless smaller bodies, and the ones left over still surround us: rocky asteroids, icy comets, and dwarf planets like Pluto. As leftover building material, they are some of the best records we have of how the Solar System began.
</KeyIdea>

Many of these objects you can watch for yourself — a bright comet, or a meteor shower on a clear night. Which raises the practical question the next lesson takes up: how do you actually find your way around the sky?
