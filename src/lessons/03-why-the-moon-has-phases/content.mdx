The Moon looks like it changes shape through the month — a sliver, a half, a full disc, then back again. It does neither of those things. The Moon is always the same ball of rock, and the Sun always lights exactly half of it. What changes is how much of that lit half we can see from Earth.

## The Moon is always half-lit

Sunlight falls on the Moon the same way it falls on Earth: one side faces the Sun and is bright, the other faces away and is dark. That is true every day of the month. A phase is simply the slice of the Moon's daylight side that happens to be turned toward us as the Moon moves along its orbit.

<KeyIdea>
The phases are a matter of viewing angle, not shadow. Earth's shadow plays no part in ordinary phases — that only appears during an eclipse. Month to month, you are watching the same half-lit Moon from a steadily changing direction.
</KeyIdea>

## A cycle of 29.5 days

The Moon takes about 27.3 days to orbit Earth once against the background stars. But the phases run on a slightly longer clock — the <Term def="The time from one new Moon to the next, about 29.5 days. It is longer than the orbit because Earth is also moving around the Sun, so the Moon must travel a little farther to line up the same way again.">synodic month</Term> of 29.5 days.

<Readout label="Phase cycle · new to new" value="29.5" unit="days" />

The cycle runs new, waxing crescent, first quarter, waxing gibbous, full, waning gibbous, last quarter, waning crescent, and back to new. Waxing means the lit part is growing; waning means it is shrinking.

## Why "first quarter" looks like a half

<Aside>
A first-quarter Moon shows a half-lit disc, which sounds like it ought to be called a half Moon. The name counts the orbit, not the light: at first quarter the Moon is one quarter of the way through its cycle. We happen to see half of its face, because we are looking at the lit side edge-on.
</Aside>

## Why we only ever see one face

The Moon spins on its axis in exactly the time it takes to orbit Earth — a balance called <Term def="When a body's rotation period matches its orbital period, so it keeps the same face toward its partner. Earth's gravity locked the Moon this way long ago.">tidal locking</Term>. The result is that the same side always faces us. The far side is not dark — it gets just as much sunlight — it simply never points our way.

## Eclipses are the rare exception

If phases came from Earth's shadow, we would get a lunar eclipse every full Moon and a solar eclipse every new Moon. We don't, because the Moon's orbit is tilted about 5° from Earth's path around the Sun. Most months the Moon passes a little above or below the line of shadows. Only when it crosses that line at the right moment do the three bodies align: Earth's shadow on the Moon, a lunar eclipse at full Moon; or the Moon's shadow on Earth, a solar eclipse at new Moon.

That tilt is why eclipses feel special — they are the few times each year when the geometry lines up exactly.
