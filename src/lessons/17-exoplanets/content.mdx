For all of human history, the only planets anyone knew orbited our own Sun. Then, in 1995, astronomers confirmed a planet circling another Sun-like star. Since then the count has climbed past five thousand, and the picture has flipped entirely: planets are not rare. Most stars have them.

## A planet around another star

An <Term def="A planet that orbits a star other than the Sun. The first around a Sun-like star was confirmed in 1995; thousands are now known.">exoplanet</Term> is simply a planet beyond our Solar System. Finding them sounds impossible — a planet is tiny, dim, and lost in the glare of a star trillions of kilometres away. We almost never see one directly. Instead, we catch them by their effects on the star.

## How to find a world you cannot see

Two methods have found the vast majority. The first is the <Term def="Detecting a planet by the small, regular dip in a star's brightness each time the planet passes in front of it from our point of view.">transit method</Term>: if a planet's orbit is lined up so it crosses in front of its star, it blocks a tiny sliver of light, and the star dims slightly and regularly. The size of the dip reveals the planet's size; its rhythm reveals the orbit. Space telescopes like Kepler and TESS have found thousands this way.

The second is the wobble, or <Term def="Detecting a planet by the tiny back-and-forth wobble its gravity causes in its star, measured as small shifts in the star's spectrum.">radial velocity</Term> method: a planet's gravity tugs its star into a small back-and-forth motion, which shows up as a rhythmic shift in the star's spectrum — the same splitting of light used to read what stars are made of.

## A zoo of strange worlds

The planets we have found are often nothing like home. There are <Term def="A giant planet like Jupiter but orbiting extremely close to its star, whipping around in days and roasting at thousands of degrees.">hot Jupiters</Term>, gas giants roasting so close to their star that a year lasts only days, and a whole class of "super-Earths" and "mini-Neptunes" — sizes between Earth and Neptune that are common elsewhere yet absent from our own system.

<FunFact>
The very first exoplanets ever found, in 1992, orbited a pulsar — the dead, spinning core left behind by an exploded star. Some of the earliest worlds we discovered circle a stellar corpse, bathed in radiation.
</FunFact>

## Worlds in the habitable zone

Most exciting are the rocky planets that sit in their star's habitable zone, at the right distance for liquid water. The TRAPPIST-1 system packs several Earth-sized planets into such a band, and Proxima b orbits the closest star to the Sun, just 4.2 light-years away. These are the worlds whose atmospheres we now try to read for signs of life.

<FunFact>
Planets appear to be the rule, not the exception: most stars host at least one. That means our galaxy alone likely contains billions of planets — quite possibly more planets than there are stars.
</FunFact>

<KeyIdea>
We rarely see exoplanets directly. We infer them from tiny, repeating dips in a star's light or from the gentle wobble of its motion. That patient, indirect detective work has revealed a galaxy brimming with worlds — including rocky planets in the habitable zone, where conditions might allow life.
</KeyIdea>

Among billions of worlds, one question grows harder to ignore: could any of them be alive? That is where we turn next.
