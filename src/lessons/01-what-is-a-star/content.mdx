A star is the easiest thing in the sky to take for granted and one of the hardest to picture honestly. The nearest one is 150 million kilometres away, yet close enough to burn your skin in an afternoon. It is a ball of incandescent gas, held together by its own gravity, generating its own light. Almost everything in this course — the planets, the seasons, the atoms in your body — traces back to a star.

## What a star actually is

A star is a sphere of plasma: gas so hot that its atoms have been torn apart into nuclei and loose electrons. It is massive enough that its own gravity crushes the core to enormous pressure and temperature. At the Sun's centre the temperature reaches about 15 million °C. That heat is not left over from how the star formed — the star makes it continuously, through <Term def="The merging of light atomic nuclei into heavier ones, which releases energy. It is the power source of every star.">nuclear fusion</Term>.

The Sun is not solid anywhere — there is no surface to land on, only gas growing denser with depth. By mass it is roughly three-quarters hydrogen and one-quarter helium, with barely a percent of everything else: carbon, oxygen, iron, the same elements that make up rock and people. Those heavier atoms were not made here. They were forged inside earlier stars and scattered when those stars died, long before the Sun existed.

<FunFact>
The Sun is so large that about 1.3 million Earths would fit inside it — and yet, as stars go, it is thoroughly average. Some red supergiants are so vast that, dropped where the Sun sits, their surface would reach out past the orbit of Jupiter.
</FunFact>

## Why a star shines

In the core, hydrogen nuclei are forced together to make helium. The helium weighs very slightly less than the hydrogen that formed it, and that missing mass leaves as energy — the relationship Einstein wrote as E = mc². Because the speed of light is so large, a minute amount of mass becomes a tremendous amount of energy.

The Sun fuses roughly 600 million tonnes of hydrogen every second, converting about 4 million tonnes of it into pure energy. It has done this for 4.6 billion years and is only about halfway through its fuel.

<FunFact>
A particle of light made in the Sun's core does not escape in a straight line. It is absorbed and re-emitted so many times by the dense gas above it that the journey to the surface takes on the order of 100,000 years. The final sprint across open space to your eye then takes just over eight minutes.
</FunFact>

<KeyIdea>
A star is a controlled balance. Gravity pulls every part of it inward; the energy pouring out of fusion pushes outward. While the two stay matched — a state called hydrostatic equilibrium — the star is stable. When the fuel runs low and fusion falters, gravity wins, and the star's life changes for good.
</KeyIdea>

## Why stars come in colours

Stars are not all like the Sun. A star's <Term def="A star's surface temperature sets its colour and brightness. Together these sort stars into spectral types, running from hot blue stars to cool red ones.">surface temperature</Term> sets its colour — and the order runs opposite to intuition.

<Aside>
Red feels hot and blue feels cold, but for stars it is reversed. The hottest stars glow blue-white at 30,000 °C or more; the coolest glow dull red at around 3,000 °C. It is the same physics as a flame, where the blue base is hotter than the orange tip. The Sun, near 5,500 °C, sits in between as a yellow-white star.
</Aside>

Temperature also tracks mass. Heavier stars run hotter, brighter, and bluer; lighter stars are cooler, dimmer, and redder. Our Sun is an ordinary middle case — formally a G-type main-sequence star, the steady hydrogen-burning stage where stars spend most of their lives.

## How long stars live

Mass decides lifespan, and not in the direction you might guess. The most massive stars shine so fiercely that they exhaust their fuel in just a few million years. The smallest red stars sip theirs so slowly they can last for trillions of years — far longer than the current age of the universe. The Sun's ten-billion-year run is, again, unremarkable.

The Sun's own future is already written. In about five billion years its core hydrogen will run low, and it will swell into a <Term def="A dying star that has run low on core fuel and ballooned to many times its original size, its surface cooling to a red glow. The Sun will become one, large enough to swallow the inner planets.">red giant</Term> large enough to swallow Mercury and Venus, perhaps Earth too. How stars end their lives — quietly or in an explosion that briefly outshines a galaxy — is a story we come back to later in the course.

That ordinariness is the point. Understand the Sun, and you understand most of the stars you can see. But the Sun up close is far from the calm lamp it appears to be — before we leave it, we look at what its restless surface is actually doing.
